Let’s start with the fundamental science: the greenhouse effect. Our Earth is wrapped in an atmosphere containing trace gases like carbon dioxide, methane, and nitrous oxide. When sunlight hits the Earth, the surface absorbs the energy and radiates it back as infrared heat. These greenhouse gases act like a thermal blanket, trapping that heat and keeping our planet habitable. However, human activities—primarily burning fossil fuels—have drastically increased these gas concentrations. 

To put this into perspective, atmospheric carbon dioxide recently reached an unprecedented 427 parts per million—the highest level in at least 800,000 years. Because carbon dioxide can remain in the atmosphere for centuries, the heat it traps accumulates over time. As a result, the global mean near-surface temperature in 2024 reached a staggering 1.55 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels, making it the warmest year in the 175-year observational record. 

We are altering the planet’s energy balance, pushing our climate system into a critical and unpredictable phase. But what does this extra heat actually do to our Earth's physical systems? ### **SECTION 2: Body Paragraph 1 – Physical Impacts: Oceans and the Cryosphere**
**Speaker 2: [Name]**

**[Acknowledge Speaker 1, step forward, and use hand gestures to emphasize key data points.]**

"Thank you, [Speaker 1's Name]. When we talk about global warming, we often focus on air temperature, but the true shock absorber of our planet is the ocean. The oceans have absorbed over 90 percent of the excess heat trapped by greenhouse gases. In fact, ocean heat content recently reached the highest level in the 65-year observational record, and the rate of ocean warming has more than doubled over the past two decades. 

This massive absorption of heat and carbon dioxide has severe physical consequences. First, as water warms, it expands. This thermal expansion, combined with the rapid melting of the Earth's cryosphere—the frozen parts of our planet like the Greenland and Antarctic ice sheets—is driving dangerous sea-level rise. Global mean sea level has hit record highs, accelerating to more than twice the rate observed just two decades ago. 

Furthermore, as the oceans absorb roughly 20 to 30 percent of human-made carbon dioxide emissions, the water undergoes a chemical change known as ocean acidification. The pH of surface waters is declining, which threatens the ability of marine organisms to build their shells. 

Finally, this trapped energy supercharges our atmosphere. It modifies the frequency and intensity of extreme weather events. Science shows that human-induced climate change is creating heavier rainfall, more intense hurricanes, and longer drought periods. But how do these physical disruptions translate to the living world? [Speaker 3's Name] will now walk us through the ecological and agricultural impacts."

To put it simply, every additional degree of global warming reduces the world's ability to produce food by about 120 calories per person per day. Heat stress and droughts are dragging down yields and making food more expensive and harder to access.
